How we get our data

From satellite and buoy to a 3.0 km forecast.

Amphitrite combines high-resolution ocean currents, waves, and wind into one operational picture — available as the browser-based Ocean Bulletin platform or as a MetOcean API. The current model is what sets it apart:

1
Observation ingestion — satellite altimetry (SWOT + nadir), infrared SST, chlorophyll, and 165,000+ in-situ drifter and buoy measurements across the North Atlantic and South Africa.
2
HIRES-CURRENTS — our proprietary deep-learning AI current model, developed with CNRS and École Polytechnique and trained end-to-end on observational data. It resolves the mesoscale eddies, current veins, and tidal signatures that numerical models miss — at 3.0 km, 14-day horizon, global. Validated against those 165,000+ independent in-situ measurements.
3
Wind & wave integration — ECMWF IFS (1/4°, hourly) wind; ECMWF WAM and ERA5 wave; regional tidal models (FES2014 global; AMM15 for the English Channel; Puertos del Estado for Gibraltar). All variables on one common grid.
4
Delivery — wind, waves, and currents updated hourly, in Ocean Bulletin or via API (GPX, CSV, XML, JSON) compatible with major navigation and VMS systems. Hindcast, reanalysis, and forecast from one unified source.

The data gap

Wind and wave forecasting is mature. Ocean current forecasting hasn't reached the same standard — until now.

Wind forecasting has achieved near-operational accuracy at 1/4° resolution globally. Wave models follow directly from wind. But ocean surface currents are a fundamentally different problem: eddies are an order of magnitude smaller than atmospheric storms, and there are far fewer ocean measurements than atmospheric ones.

70% of ship captains rate ocean current forecasts as average or poor — compared to just 4% for wind. That gap is what Amphitrite closes.

Standard operational models (EU Copernicus, US NOAA) often disagree significantly on surface currents — predicting different locations and intensities for the same current vein on the same day. Misplacing a current vein by 30–50 nautical miles changes the routing decision entirely.

Where it fits

Where it fits into maritime operations.

Route optimisation — all three variables
Optimise passage plans against wind, waves, and ocean currents at 3.0 km. Plan routes that exploit favourable currents, avoid counter-currents, and select weather windows — all in one platform.
Offshore workability windows
DSV, crane vessel, and construction vessel ops depend on wave height, period, and current speed. Get accurate go/no-go decisions days in advance — not just wind-based estimates.
DP-sensitive operations
Accurate ocean current data at the operational site reduces thruster demand, improves DP station-keeping, and extends operational weather windows — particularly in areas with strong tidal or eddy currents.
Tow and rig move planning
Tug-and-tow operations are highly sensitive to current forces across the towline. Forecast currents at 3.0 km along the entire route, not just at waypoints — critical for loaded semi-sub and jacket tows.
Port approach and coastal passages
High-resolution tidal and coastal current data for approaches to Gibraltar, the English Channel, and major chokepoints — where standard models degrade to unusable resolution.
Fuel and emissions management
Current-informed routing reduces fuel consumption directly — both by exploiting favourable currents and avoiding headwinds created by counter-current conditions. Validated fuel savings from AIS data on real voyages.
Voyage risk reduction and ETA accuracy
14-day forecast at 3.0 km improves ETA confidence, reduces unexpected delays, and gives fleet managers a consistent, accurate picture across the fleet — not dependent on individual ship's officer judgment.

Common questions

Practical answers for maritime teams.

Do we still need a specialist weather routing service alongside this? +

Ocean Bulletin works alongside existing weather routing services as a high-resolution current data layer — or as a standalone routing tool where you're building the capability in-house. Most teams use it to augment decisions their routing officers already make, particularly on ocean crossings where current variability is highest.

Is this only relevant for shipping? +

No — active users include offshore construction (Boskalis, Heerema), subsea and cable lay (Orange Marine), defence and naval operations (NATO), research vessels, and superyachts. Anywhere that ocean currents, waves, or wind affect operational decisions.

Can this integrate with our existing systems? +

Yes. The MetOcean API delivers data in multiple formats including GPX, CSV, XML, JSON, and more — compatible with all major navigation systems, VMS platforms, and voyage management tools. Route data can be pulled for any set of waypoints and timestamps via a single API call.

What areas are covered — including offshore and coastal? +

Global ocean coverage (60°S to 65°N), including high-resolution tidal layers for the Strait of Gibraltar (1/45°), the English Channel (AMM15, 1.5 km), and major coastal chokepoints. Bathymetry and SST layers are also available globally.

How do we start — is there a pilot option? +

Yes — a vessel profile and route study is the standard starting point. This establishes a custom performance model for a specific vessel and validates the data against your own voyage history. Get in touch to discuss scope and timing.
